Generalized linear mixed models

As an alternative to the preregistered analyses, we used generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) to test our hypotheses. GLMMs provide advantages over the originally planned ANOVAs as they allow to:

  • Add random effects for both participants and stimuli, which allows more of the error to be modeled;
  • Fit the data of individual trials instead of the means for each participant, which gives more statistical power;
  • Accommodate missing data, so we could include data of all participants irrespective of the number excluded trials;
  • Test the effect of continuous variables (e.g., AQ), so both of our hypotheses can be tested with a single model;
  • Specify the distribution of the dependent variable, which avoids the need of non-linear transformations of reaction time data to adhere to model assumptions of ANOVA and linear-mixed models.
# PREPARE DATASET FOR GLMM (trial data)

Model specification

To test our hypotheses, we fitted single-trial data to the following model:

Condensed form:
  DV ~ Imitative*Gaze*AQ + Spatial*Gaze*AQ + (1|ID) + (1|Stimulus)

Extended form:
  DV ~ Imitative + Spatial + Gaze + AQ + Imitative:Gaze + Spatial:Gaze + Imitative:AQ + 
    Spatial:AQ + Gaze:AQ + Imitative:Gaze:AQ + Spatial:Gaze:AQ + (1|ID) + (1|Stimulus)

Separate models were ran for RT and ER data (dependent variables).

Fixed effects

According to our hypotheses, the main fixed effects of interest were:

  1. Main effects of Imitative and Spatial, which would indicate the occurrence of compatibility effects;
  2. The 2-way interactions Imitative:Gaze and Spatial:Gaze, which would show that the degree of the corresponding compatibility effects depends on whether the target directed the gaze towards or away from the participant (Hypothesis 1);
  3. The 3-way interactions Imitative:Gaze:AQ and Spatial:Gaze:AQ, which would suggest that autistic traits modulate the influence of gaze on imitative or spatial compatibility (Hypothesis 2).

Note that we did not include any 2-, 3- or 4-way interactions containing Spatial:Imitative as we did not have any a-priori hypothesis about interaction effects between spatial and imitative compatibility. Including these predictors in the model also led to further issues with model convergence and singular fit.

Effect coding contrasts were set for all categorical predictors (i.e.Â Imitative, Spatial, Gaze). The continuous predictor (AQ) was mean-centered.

Random effects

The random effects structure included random intercepts for participants (1|ID) and stimulus (1|Stimulus). The levels of Stimulus correspond to each of the possible combinations of the actors presented in the gaze clips, the direction of their head movement (turn towards the right side, turn towards the left side), the finger that the actor lifted (index finger, middle finger) and the color of the imperative cue (green, purple).

DV distribution and link function

Reaction times

To select the response distribution that best fitted the observed RT data, we compared GLMMs with Gaussian distribution, Gamma distribution and Inverse Gaussian distribution. The latter two represent right skewed unimodal distributions with continuous responses greater than or equal to 0, which make them good candidates to approximate the observed RT responses (Lo & Andrews, 2015). All models used the Identity link function, as we assume that our manipulations linearly affected the RT, in line with mental chronometry perspectives.

Visual inspection of the residual plots showed that both the Gamma and Inverse Gaussian distribution had lower heteroscedasity as compared to the Gaussian distribution.

# model
formula <- formula(rt ~ imitative*gaze*aq + spatial*gaze*aq + (1|id) + (1|stimulus))

# gaussian distribution, identity link (raw rt)
m.gaus <- lmer(formula, data=d.trial.rt, REML=FALSE)

# gamma distribution, identity link (raw rt)
m.gamma <- glmer(formula, data=d.trial.rt, family=Gamma(link='identity'),
                   control=glmerControl(optimizer='bobyqa', optCtrl=list(maxfun=2e5)))

# inverse gaussian distribution, identity link (raw rt)
m.invgaus <- glmer(formula, data=d.trial.rt, family=inverse.gaussian(link='identity'),
                   control=glmerControl(optimizer='bobyqa', optCtrl=list(maxfun=2e5)))

A comparison of the three models on the basis of AIC and BIC values indicated that the Inverse Gaussian distribution is the one that approximates best to the observed RT distribution. Therefore, we used the inverse gaussian distribution with identity link function for the GLMM with RT data.

Error rates

For the GLMM with ER data, we used a binomial distribution (mixed-effect logistic regression) to conform to the binary nature of the dependent variable (correct, incorrect response).

The significant main effect of gaze indicates that responses following averted gaze were slower than following direct gaze.

3-way interaction

The significant 3-way interaction between Imitative:Gaze:AQ suggests that the influence of gaze on imitative compatibility varied as a function of autistic traits. To follow-up the 3-way interaction, we tested the conditional effects of AQ on imitative compatibility for direct gaze and averted gaze conditions separately.

Results from this GLMM indicated that the difference in RTs between imitatively incompatible and imitatively compatible trials (i.e., imitative effect) following averted gaze increased with the level of autistic traits. For direct gaze, however, the slopes for the effect of AQ on imitative compatible and incompatible conditions did not significantly differ.

General compatibility

To make our results comparable to previous automatic imitation studies in which imitative and spatial compatibility were confounded, we ran an additional GLMM on RT data of the subset of trials in which spatial and imitative compatibility overlapped (i.e.Â left-hand trials). The to test the effects on general compatibility, GLMM included the following fixed and random effects:

Condensed form: RT ~ GeneralCompt*Gaze*AQ + (1|ID) + (1|Stimulus)

Extended form: RT ~ GeneralCompt + Gaze + AQ + GeneralCompt:Gaze + GeneralCompt:AQ + Gaze:AQ + GeneralCompt:Gaze:AQ + (1|ID) + (1|Stimulus)

Effect coding contrasts were set for categorical predictors, and AQ was mean-centered.
